Tune Up

Tune Up Your Breaststroke

By JEFF COMMINGS

In this Column: 

  • Main Set
  • Ask the Coach
  • From the Center Lanes

Main Set

In the January-February 2019 issue of SWIMMER, I highlighted the importance of adding vertical kick to your training regimen as a way to strengthen your legs and your core. The set I offered was very simple: 15 seconds of vertical kick, followed by a fast 25 swim and a 100 recovery. Repeating that three more times makes the legs burn, and helps you push through the type of pain you’ll experience at the end of a 100-yard race.

I want to take this set one step further, to not only push your limits but to help you replicate the type of lactic acid buildup you experience at the end of a 100-yard race. Kicking for 15 seconds helps, but doubling that will intensify your training.

The tough part of this set is not the 30 seconds of vertical kicking. It comes in the final 25 yards of the fast swim. Pushing off the wall is likely to increase the buildup of lactic acid, and help train your mind to push past the pain and finish the swim with proper technique and strong kick. For those who find themselves barely kicking at the end of a 100 or 200 freestyle race, this will give you the confidence to create that powerful kick at the end of the race that will vanquish your competition.

I have done all four rounds of this set focusing on only one stroke, and I have also done this in IM order. You can make it as varied as you like, but never try to back off on the vertical kick in order to have the energy to finish the fast swim. Having tired legs when you push off for the fast swim is required!

Ask the Coach



Breaststroke training often focuses on the kick, as it’s a kick-driven stroke. But Arizona Masters Swim Club Coach Brigid Bunch has her swimmers focus on both their pull and their kick during breaststroke sets.

She recommends doing breaststroke pull sets whether you’re an elite breaststroker or someone who struggles to kick breaststroke. “I love working on the feel of the water during the scull,” Bunch says. She recommends not using a pull buoy when pulling because it hinders your body alignment.

Bunch often also creates a breaststroke kick set so her swimmers can work on “riding the glide,” or staying in a streamlined position to help conserve energy in the 200 breaststroke. She asks her swimmers to focus on finishing their kick: bringing their feet together at the end to get every bit of propulsion out of each kick.

From the Center Lanes



North Carolina Masters Swimming member Sara Dunn knows the 200 breaststroke can be a painful event, but she’s found the key to success: her perfect racing plan.

She builds the race to have the capacity to finish strong by increasing her stroke rate as the race progresses. This leads to the five-time USMS All-American having an aggressive final 50 as other swimmers might be feeling the burn.

“For me, it’s only a 50 left in the race,” says Dunn, 49, who holds 58 USMS individual Top 10 times and swam breaststroke on three USMS-record-setting relays. “I can do anything for 50 more yards. I’m not a sprinter, and the 200 allows me to stretch it out. There’s a little more strategy in the 200.”
